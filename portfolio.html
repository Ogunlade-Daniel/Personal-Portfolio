I started as a frontend developer who got curious about what happened after the form submitted. That curiosity turned into a habit of following problems wherever they actually live — which is rarely just one layer of the stack.

These days I move comfortably between designing a component system in Javascript, writing the Python or Django service behind it, modeling the Database schema, and configuring the pipeline that ships it to production. I care less about "frontend vs backend" and more about whether the thing works, holds up under load, and is pleasant to use.

Outside of client work, I contribute to a couple of open-source tooling projects and spend an unreasonable amount of time tuning my terminal setup — some habits the eyebrow above doesn't lie about.

If you need someone who can own a feature end-to-end and explain the tradeoffs in plain language, that's the role I'm best at.
